## Preventive vs. Predictive Maintenance: Definitions

**Preventive maintenance** is a calendar-based approach where maintenance tasks are performed at scheduled intervals — monthly filter changes, quarterly HVAC inspections, annual equipment tune-ups — regardless of whether equipment currently shows problems. The goal is to service equipment before it fails.

**Predictive maintenance** is a data-driven approach where sensors monitor equipment condition in real time — vibration, temperature, energy consumption, humidity, airflow — and analytics predict when failures are likely to occur. Maintenance is performed when the data indicates it's needed, not on a fixed calendar.

Both approaches prevent equipment failures. The difference is **when and why** maintenance happens.

## How Each Approach Works

### Preventive Maintenance: The Calendar Approach

1. An HVAC tech visits quarterly to inspect your rooftop unit
2. They change filters, check refrigerant, clean coils, and lubricate bearings
3. The work happens every 90 days regardless of equipment condition
4. If the compressor was going to fail in 30 days, the quarterly visit catches early warning signs

### Predictive Maintenance: The Data Approach

1. A vibration sensor on your compressor motor detects increasing oscillation
2. An energy monitor shows the compressor drawing 15% more power than baseline
3. The system alerts your maintenance provider: "Compressor bearing degradation detected — estimated 45 days to failure"
4. A technician replaces the bearing during normal hours at normal rates — no emergency, no downtime

## The Key Differences

| Factor | Preventive | Predictive |
|--------|:----------:|:----------:|
| **Trigger** | Calendar/schedule | Sensor data/analytics |
| **Approach** | "Service it before it breaks" | "Service it when data says it's needed" |
| **Equipment uptime** | 85–95% | 95–99% |
| **Unnecessary maintenance** | 30–40% of visits (equipment was fine) | 5–10% |
| **Cost to implement** | Low ($800–$3,000/mo) | Medium–High (sensors + monitoring) |
| **Technical requirement** | None (managed by provider) | IoT sensors + analytics platform |
| **Best for** | All buildings | High-value equipment, critical systems |
| **Industry adoption** | 80%+ of commercial buildings | ~15% (growing rapidly) |

Source: Deloitte "Predictive Maintenance" report, 2024; IFMA benchmarking survey

## When Preventive Maintenance Is Enough

Preventive maintenance is sufficient — and optimal — for most small commercial buildings when:

- **Equipment age is under 15 years** — Newer systems fail predictably and respond well to scheduled service
- **System complexity is low** — Standard rooftop HVAC units, packaged systems, split systems
- **Building is under 50,000 sq ft** — The cost of sensor deployment exceeds the value for small systems
- **No critical uptime requirements** — An HVAC outage is uncomfortable but not catastrophic (unlike in a hospital or data center)
- **Budget prioritizes breadth over depth** — PM covers HVAC + cleaning + pest + floors; predictive only covers monitored equipment

For a 15,000 sq ft medical office or retail space, preventive maintenance prevents **75–80% of equipment failures** at a cost of $800–$3,000/month. That's the right trade-off for most small building owners.

## When Predictive Maintenance Adds Value

Predictive maintenance justifies its additional cost when:

- **Equipment replacement cost is high** — Compressor failure: $5,000–$15,000. Sensor that prevents it: $100–$300
- **Downtime has direct revenue impact** — Restaurant can't serve food without HVAC; medical office can't see patients
- **Equipment is aging** — Systems over 15 years old fail less predictably; sensors catch what schedules miss
- **You have remote or hard-to-access equipment** — Rooftop units, crawl-space equipment, multi-story risers
- **Regulatory environments demand documentation** — Healthcare, food service, pharmaceutical

## The Best Approach: Predictive + Preventive

The highest-performing buildings use both approaches as complementary layers:

| Layer | What It Does | Cost | Coverage |
|-------|-------------|:----:|---------|
| **Preventive (foundation)** | Scheduled service for all building systems | $800–$3,000/mo | Everything: HVAC, cleaning, pest, floors, handyman |
| **Predictive (enhancement)** | Sensor monitoring for critical equipment | $200–$800/mo | High-value equipment: compressors, motors, water systems |

**Combined**, these layers deliver:
- 95–99% equipment uptime (vs. 85–95% for preventive alone)
- 40–60% reduction in total maintenance costs vs. reactive approaches
- Real-time alerts for critical issues between scheduled visits
- Complete documentation for compliance and insurance

## The Predictive Maintenance Roadmap for Small Buildings

Not ready for the full predictive stack? Here's a phased approach:

**Phase 1 — Now:** Start a preventive maintenance program. This is the foundation — no predictive system works without scheduled baseline maintenance.

**Phase 2 — Month 3:** Add water/moisture sensors to critical areas (near water heaters, under sinks, in mechanical rooms). Cost: $100–$300. These catch the most expensive surprise failures.

**Phase 3 — Month 6:** Add vibration and energy monitors to your most critical HVAC equipment (oldest units, highest-value zones). Cost: $300–$800.

**Phase 4 — Year 2:** Evaluate full predictive coverage based on data from the first year. By then, you'll know which equipment gives you trouble and deserves monitoring.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between predictive and preventive maintenance?

Preventive maintenance is calendar-based — services are performed at scheduled intervals (monthly, quarterly, annually) regardless of equipment condition. Predictive maintenance is data-driven — sensors monitor equipment in real time and analytics predict when failures are likely, triggering maintenance only when needed. Preventive maintenance prevents 75–80% of equipment failures at low cost; predictive maintenance prevents 90–95% of failures but requires sensor investment. Most commercial buildings benefit from preventive maintenance as the foundation, with predictive monitoring added for high-value or critical equipment.

### Is predictive maintenance better than preventive maintenance?

Predictive maintenance achieves higher equipment uptime (95–99% vs. 85–95%) and reduces unnecessary maintenance visits by 30–40% compared to preventive. However, it requires IoT sensors, a monitoring platform, and higher upfront investment. For small commercial buildings under 50,000 sq ft, preventive maintenance typically delivers better ROI because it covers all building systems (not just monitored equipment) at lower cost. The optimal approach is preventive maintenance as the foundation with predictive monitoring added for critical equipment like HVAC compressors and water systems.

### How much does predictive maintenance cost for a small building?

Predictive maintenance sensor deployment for a small commercial building (10,000–25,000 sq ft) costs $500–$2,000 for hardware (vibration sensors, energy monitors, moisture detectors, temperature/humidity sensors) plus $200–$800/month for monitoring and analytics. This is in addition to — not a replacement for — a preventive maintenance program ($800–$3,000/month). Total combined cost: $1,000–$3,800/month. For comparison, enterprise building automation systems that include predictive analytics cost $50,000–$500,000 to install.

### What sensors are used in predictive maintenance?

Common predictive maintenance sensors for commercial buildings include: vibration sensors ($50–$200) that detect bearing degradation in motors and compressors 30–90 days before failure; energy monitors ($100–$300) that identify compressor strain and refrigerant loss through abnormal power consumption; temperature and humidity sensors ($30–$100) that detect HVAC underperformance and mold risk; water and moisture sensors ($30–$80) that catch leaks before they cause structural damage; and airflow sensors ($80–$200) that measure duct velocity to identify filter clogging and ductwork issues. NFC verification tags ($1–$3 each) complement these sensors by documenting that scheduled maintenance was physically completed.

### Can small buildings use predictive maintenance?

Yes. Modern IoT sensors have made predictive maintenance affordable for small commercial buildings. A basic predictive monitoring setup costs $500–$2,000 in hardware — compared to $50,000–$500,000 for enterprise building automation systems. The key is selective deployment: rather than monitoring every system, small buildings should add sensors to their most critical and expensive-to-replace equipment (HVAC compressors, water heaters, and condensate systems). This targeted approach, combined with a preventive maintenance program for routine services, delivers 95–99% equipment uptime at a fraction of enterprise BAS cost.
