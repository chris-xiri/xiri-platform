## You Liked Last Month's Crew. This Month's Crew Doesn't Know Where the Supply Closet Is.

You finally had a crew you trusted. Maria knew which conference room to clean first. Jose understood the medical waste protocol. They were thorough, consistent, and quiet.

Then one Monday, two strangers showed up with your cleaning company's shirts on. Nobody told you. Nobody introduced them. And the next morning, the break room looked like it hadn't been touched.

Welcome to the cleaning industry's turnover problem.

## The Numbers Are Brutal

The [Building Service Contractors Association International (BSCAI)](https://www.bscai.org/) reports that commercial cleaning has an average annual employee turnover rate of **200-300%**. That means a 10-person cleaning company replaces its entire workforce 2-3 times per year.

For your building, this means:

| Crew Size | Avg. Tenure per Employee | Your Crew Fully Turns Over Every... |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| 1 person | 4-6 months | 4-6 months |
| 2 people | 4-6 months | 2-3 months (staggered) |
| 3 people | 4-6 months | Almost continuously |

If you have a 2-person crew, at least one of them is statistically being replaced every 2-3 months.

## Why Turnover Is So High

### 1. Low Pay, Physical Work
Cleaning is hard physical labor at near-minimum wage. The median hourly wage for janitors and building cleaners is [$16.20/hour](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/building-and-grounds-cleaning/janitors-and-building-cleaners.htm) nationally. Workers leave for any job that pays a dollar more.

### 2. Night Shifts Are Hard to Staff
Most commercial cleaning happens between 6 PM and 6 AM. Night work is isolating, exhausting, and incompatible with family life. Many workers take the job temporarily and leave when something better opens up.

### 3. No Career Path
There's no promotion ladder in most cleaning companies. A cleaner today is a cleaner in 3 years. Motivated workers move on.

### 4. Poor Management
Small cleaning companies often have one owner managing 15-30 accounts with minimal support. Crews feel unsupervised, undervalued, and interchangeable — because to the company, they are.

## What Turnover Means for Your Building

Each time your crew turns over, you lose:

- **Building knowledge** — Which doors to lock, which alarms to set, which areas need extra attention
- **Routine consistency** — The new crew doesn't know your Thursday executive meeting means the boardroom needs priority cleaning
- **Trust and rapport** — You knew Maria by name. Now you're starting from zero with someone who may not last the month
- **Quality** — New crew members are less thorough until they learn the building. Many are gone before they do

## How to Protect Your Building From the Churn

### Accept That Turnover Will Happen — Then Build Systems Around It

You can't fix the cleaning industry's turnover problem. But you can make your building's cleaning quality independent of who's holding the mop.

**1. Insist on documented cleaning scopes** — A room-by-room, task-by-task scope means any new crew member knows exactly what to do on night one.

**2. Require shift-level verification** — When each shift is verified with [NFC proof of work](/solutions/nfc-proof-of-work), crew changes don't create accountability gaps. The new person scans the same tags, completes the same checklists.

**3. Separate cleaning from quality management** — When an independent party verifies the work (like XIRI's Facility Solutions Managers), quality stays consistent even as crews rotate.

**4. Demand to be notified of crew changes** — If your cleaning company swaps crews without telling you, that's a red flag. You should know who has after-hours access to your building.
